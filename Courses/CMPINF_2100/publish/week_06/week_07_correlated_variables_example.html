Uncorrelated vs Highly Correlated vs Anti-Correlated¶

This report generates random numbers from Multi-Variate Normal (MVN) distributions to illustrate what uncorrelated variables look like compared to highly positively correlated and "highly" negatively correlated variables. The MVN distribution is discussed in more detail in CMPINF 2120. At present, what matters is that you qualitatively understand the difference between positive correlation, negative correlation, and zero correlation.

We need the "big 4" for ths report.

Simulated data¶

We will generate random observations of 2 variables. Each variable will have a marginal standard normal distribution. The standard normal is a special name to a Gaussian with mean 0 and standard deviation 1. We will generate 100 random observations of the 2 variables.

Define the mean vector as a 1D NumPy array of zeros.

In [3]:

mean_vector = np.zeros( num_x )

Uncorrelated variables¶

Let's first generate random observations for uncorrelated normally distributed variables. The covariance matrix for the uncorrelated or zero correlation case is defined below.

In [4]:
cov_matrix_rho_zero = np.identity( num_x )

The covariance matrix has as many rows and columns as variables.

The uncorrelated case has a covariance matrix with two sets of terms. The main diagonal has ONES because the standard normal assumes the marginal variance is 1. The off-diagonal terms are ZERO because the covariance is zero.

IMPORTANT: Right now the primary point is NOT how the random numbers were generated from the Multivariate Normal (MVN) random number generator. CMPINF 2120 discusses the MVN in much more detail. The main point right now is that you are working with 2 continuous variables which were created with ZERO correlation.

Let's explore the marginal distributions and the relationships between the two variables with a pairs plot. The marginal histograms reveal the distributions are roughly symmetric around zero. How would you describe the scatter plots? Is there a clear direction or trend between the two variables? Or, are the observations seemingly randomly located?

In [11]:
sns.pairplot( data = x_rho_zero_df )

plt.show()

We know that the observations were generated assuming zero correlation between the two variables. However, let's calculate the empirical or sample correlation matrix. After all, this is what you would do when exploring data. You do not know the correlation matrix that generated the data. You must calculate it from the data that you have. As shown below the sample correlation between x01 and x02 is not identically zero, but it quite close to zero.

In [12]:
x_rho_zero_df.corr()
Out[12]:
x01 x02
x01 1.000000 0.007904
x02 0.007904 1.000000

I prefer to visualize the correlation matrix as a heat map in a corrplot! The fact that the two variables are uncorrelated is represented by the GREY off-diagonal tiles because the consistent color bar bounds of -1 and +1 are used with a diverging color palette!

In [13]:
fig, ax = plt.subplots()

sns.heatmap(data = x_rho_zero_df.corr(),
            vmin=-1, vmax=1, center=0,
            cmap='coolwarm', cbar=False,
            annot=True, annot_kws={'size': 25},
            ax=ax)

plt.show()

Thus, scatter plots that do not have a clear "direction" are associated with near zero correlation!

Highly positively correlated variables¶

However, the easiest way to understand what zero correlation "looks like" is to examine what highly correlated scatter plots look like. Let's specify the covariance matrix such that the correlation is very high between the two variables. The cell below creates a covariance matrix where the correlation coefficient is 0.9 between the two variables. Do you see how the 2D NumPy does not have zeros on the off-diagonal elements? That is because the two variables are not uncorrelated! Please note, the correlation matrix and covariance matrix are the same in this case because the two variables have marginal variances of 1.

In [14]:
cov_matrix_rho_high = np.array([[1, 0.9], [0.9, 1]])

print( cov_matrix_rho_high )

[[1.  0.9]
 [0.9 1. ]]

Let's now generate random observations using the highly correlated distribution.

In [15]:
x_rho_high_mat = rg.multivariate_normal(mean_vector, cov_matrix_rho_high, size=100)

Convert to a DataFrame using the same naming convention we used previously.

In [16]:
x_rho_high_df = pd.DataFrame(x_rho_high_mat, columns=['x%02d' % (d+1) for d in range(num_x)])

The pairs plot shows that the two variables increase together! There is a clear direction in the scatter plots! There are no observations in the lower right corner or the upper left corner.

In [17]:
sns.pairplot( data = x_rho_high_df )

plt.show()

Why are there are no observations in the lower right corner? The scatter plot is created with the Seaborn AXIS LEVEL function below. Vertical and horizontal reference lines are included to denote high positive values of x01 and low negative values of x02, respectively. The intersection of the lines forms a patch in the lower right hand corner. No observations are located within that patch! That is because the lower right hand corner consists of low x02 values and high x01 values. The highly positive correlation between the two variables means such conditions are NOT LIKELY! Large values of x01 are observed WITH large values of x02. Likewise, small values of x01 are observed with small values of x02. The two variables MOVE together. Increasing (decreasing) one variable is associated with increasing (decreasing) the other!

In [18]:
fig, ax = plt.subplots()

sns.scatterplot(data = x_rho_high_df, x='x01', y='x02', ax = ax)

ax.hlines(y=[-1, -2], xmin = ax.get_xlim()[0], xmax=ax.get_xlim()[1], color='red')

ax.vlines(x=[1, 2], ymin = ax.get_ylim()[0], ymax=ax.get_ylim()[1], color='red')

plt.show()

The sample correlation coefficient is high between the x01 and x02!

In [19]:
fig, ax = plt.subplots()

sns.heatmap(data = x_rho_high_df.corr(),
            vmin=-1, vmax=1, center=0,
            cmap='coolwarm', cbar=False,

            ax=ax)

plt.show()

Thus, clear positive relationships/trends between the variables are associated with correlation coefficients near 1.

"Highly" Negatively or Anti Correlated variables¶

Let's now see what ANTI correlation looks like. Specify the covariance matrix with anti related variables. "Anti" or "negative" relationship means that increasing one variable is associated with decreasing the other variable. The covariance matrix is specified below with -0.9 in the off-diagonal elements.

In [20]:
cov_matrix_rho_neg = np.array([[1, -0.9], [-0.9, 1]])

print( cov_matrix_rho_neg )

[[ 1.  -0.9]
 [-0.9  1. ]]

Generate the random observations.

In [21]:
x_rho_neg_mat = rg.multivariate_normal(mean_vector, cov_matrix_rho_neg, size=100)

Convert to a DataFrame.

In [22]:
x_rho_neg_df = pd.DataFrame(x_rho_neg_mat, columns=['x'+str(d+1).zfill(2) for d in range(num_x)])

The pairs plot shows that the two variables move opposite each other! There is a clear direction in the scatter plots, but that direction is DIFFERENT from what we saw with the highly positively correlated variables.

In [23]:
sns.pairplot( data = x_rho_neg_df )

plt.show()

Anti-correlated variables show that increasing values of one variable are associated with decreasing values of the other variable. Isolating the same lower right corner now reveals observations are present in the region of high x01 but low x02! The upper right corner of the plot is now "empty" because of the anti-correlation!

In [24]:
fig, ax = plt.subplots()

sns.scatterplot(data = x_rho_neg_df, x='x01', y='x02', ax = ax)

ax.hlines(y=[-1, -2], xmin = ax.get_xlim()[0], xmax=ax.get_xlim()[1], color='red')

ax.vlines(x=[1, 2], ymin = ax.get_ylim()[0], ymax=ax.get_ylim()[1], color='red')

plt.show()

The sample correlation coefficient is negative! Thus, negative correlation coefficients are associated with "negative" or "anti" trends between the two variables!
